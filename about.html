Who We Are

About Usul Learning

An independent initiative dedicated to making authentic Islamic knowledge accessible through structured, scholarly educational resources.

Our Mission

Grounded in Tradition. Clear in Presentation.

Usul Learning was established to present the foundations of Islamic worship in a clear, structured format — grounded entirely in the Qur'an, authentic Sunnah, and the Shafi'i scholarly tradition. It is an effort to make established scholarly knowledge more accessible, offered as a complement to — not a replacement for — the rich and enduring tradition of Islamic education that has always existed and continues to flourish.

The name Usul refers to "foundations" or "roots" in Arabic — reflecting the aim of this project: to build upon the authentic foundations of Islamic practice as preserved and transmitted by the scholars of Ahl al-Sunnah wal-Jamāʿah across generations.

All material is derived from the Qur'an and authentic Sunnah, presented according to the understanding of Ahl al-Sunnah wal-Jamāʿah within the Shafi'i madhab. The goal is faithful transmission of established scholarly knowledge — not personal interpretation.

Our Vision

A Resource for Every Seeking Muslim

The vision behind Usul Learning is simple: that any Muslim, wherever they are and whatever their background, can find here a grounded introduction to the practice of their faith rooted in authentic sources — and through it, be encouraged to seek further knowledge from qualified scholars and teachers.

This is a long-term project. Volume I is the beginning of a series that, in sha' Allah, will grow to cover many dimensions of Islamic worship and practice — each volume building on the last, each rooted in the same commitment to authenticity, clarity, and scholarly integrity.

About the Author

Muhammed Faheem

Muhammed Faheem is the founder and author behind Usul Learning. Committed to presenting authentic Islamic teachings in a clear and structured manner, he developed this initiative to help Muslims — especially those learning independently — deepen their understanding of worship and connect it more closely to what the Prophet ﷺ taught.

The project reflects a personal conviction: that every Muslim, wherever they are and whatever their background, deserves access to clear, grounded Islamic knowledge — and that making such a resource available is itself a form of service to the Ummah.

For feedback, corrections, or suggestions, readers are welcome to reach out at contact@usullearning.com.

Author's Note

How This Book Came to Be

I was in my late twenties when I decided I wanted to teach someone close to me how to perform wudu and salah properly. I wanted to do it well — not in a rushed or informal way, but clearly, step by step, in a way that was easy to follow and beautiful to look at. So I opened a design application and began building a slide-based guide. I thought I already knew how to perform these acts of worship. I had been practising for years. I was confident.

That confidence did not last long.

As I began researching each step carefully — reading the Qur'an, searching through hadith, cross-referencing what I had always done with what the Prophet ﷺ actually taught — I started to notice things that troubled me. Practices I had grown up with, that I had never questioned, that felt completely normal, did not always have the foundation I assumed they had. Some of what I had been doing, and what many around me were doing, had no basis in authentic hadith. Some of it was simply passed down, generation to generation, without anyone pausing to ask where it came from.

I was unsettled. Then I was determined.

I kept going deeper. What began as a simple guide to wudu and salah expanded as I realised how much foundational knowledge a Muslim needs before those acts even begin — the pillars of Islam, the meaning of Iman, the concept of Ihsan, the necessity of Ghusl, the rulings that govern each step of worship. I had not heard of some of these concepts before I started writing. I was learning as I was writing. Every chapter I completed opened a door to the next.

Over eight to ten months — through many drafts, many revisions, many long hours of reading — the work grew to fourteen chapters. Throughout that process, I was not just writing a book. I was correcting my own understanding. I was re-examining worship I had performed for years. And when the writing was finally done, the first thing I did was apply what I had learned to my own practice.

I accepted only what could be traced to authentic sources. I consulted only Sahih and Hasan hadith. I followed the Shafi'i madhhab as my scholarly framework — not as a rigid boundary, but as a structured and time-honoured methodology for understanding the Sunnah. And I chose a slide-based format for this particular work: one step per page, clear and visual, suited to the way I was thinking about the material and the kind of reader I had in mind — someone who might benefit from a structured, visual presentation of each step.

When it was done, I thought about how many people might be in the same position I was in before this journey. Practising sincerely, but carrying gaps in their knowledge they were not even aware of — not from carelessness, but simply because that is the nature of learning. We all begin somewhere. We all have blind spots until something prompts us to look more carefully.

I made the decision to offer this book free of charge. That is a personal choice for this work, and it is one I intend to honour permanently. The Islamic tradition has always been served by scholars and teachers who give of themselves generously — through their books, their lessons, and their time. This is my own small contribution, offered in that same spirit, within the limits of my ability.

I am not a scholar. I do not present myself as one. This work is now under scholarly verification — because I believe that no sincere effort in this field should go out into the world without being checked by those who are qualified to check it. Whatever is correct in this book is from Allah alone. Whatever errors remain are mine, and I ask that readers bring them to my attention so they can be corrected.

My hope is simple. That this book benefits someone the way the writing of it benefited me. That it helps a Muslim somewhere worship with a little more clarity, a little more confidence, and a little more closeness to what the Prophet ﷺ actually taught us. If Allah wills that to happen — even for one person — then every hour spent writing it was worth it.
